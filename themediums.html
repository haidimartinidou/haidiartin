How it started

With a lot of leftover blue.

It started with a set of plastic columns. I had just moved into my first apartment, living alone for the first time, and I wanted them blue. So I bought the blue paint, did the columns, and found I had far too much left over.

So I started painting other things. Random things, even words, a soft blue on a big sheet of paper. Then I painted whatever I could reach: the fridge, cardboard, anything lying around. From there I moved to canvases and painting paper, and sometimes rice paper, which I love, especially with crayons on top.

Ever since, I have grabbed whatever medium came my way. A guitar, a watercolour agenda, all of it. This page is a map of that.

The darkroom

Every roll goes to the same place. For the past five years I have developed my film, by hand, at The Great Darkroom Experience in Thessaloniki. Zizis runs it, and he is the only person I trust with a roll.

Nikon

When I'd rather chase the moment than wait for it.

The little Nikon is for the in-between. Quick, candid, digital, no roll to send away. It catches the things that would be long gone by the time a shutter on film had thought about it.

iPad

The medium I found in a book.

The iPad came from a book. On a trip to UC Berkeley I found one for sale in a campus cafe, all about making art with markers. I didn't have the markers, so I tried everything it described on an iPad instead, and I have liked the medium ever since.

It is wonderfully easy to work with, and a lot friendlier to its surroundings, whether that surrounding is the house I am living in or the many, many works I have ruined before.

Typewriter

Words, when they need somewhere to go.

Alongside the paint there have always been words. Writing is the medium I return to when something wants saying plainly, a way of watching a city and holding on to what it feels like.

Parallaxi is an independent free-press magazine based in Thessaloniki, known for documenting the city's cultural life, its public conversations, people, neighbourhoods, memory and everyday contradictions. For readers outside Greece, think of it as a local cultural magazine and alternative city paper, rooted in the identity and atmosphere of the city.

My writing there is personal essays and urban reflections on memory, grief, belonging and collective life. I have written about Thessaloniki as a place of intimacy and contradiction, the personal aftermath of public tragedy, and the emotional force of people gathering around a shared loss or a shared sense of justice. It is my way of observing cities, keeping memory, and giving language to private and collective feeling.
